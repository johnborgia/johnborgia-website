Everything As Opposite: What Happens When We Ask Different Questions

By John Borgia • February 8, 2026

The Observation

After thirty years as a composer and a decade developing strategic concepts, I've noticed something curious:

Systems optimize for what we measure.

This isn't controversial. It's basic systems design. Set a target, optimize toward it, get results aligned with that target.

The interesting part? We often measure the wrong things—not because of malice, but because some things are easier to measure than others.

Revenue is easy to measure. Human flourishing is harder.
Engagement metrics are clear. Creative fulfillment is fuzzy.
Symptom management is trackable. Root cause elimination is invisible when it works.

So systems naturally evolve toward what's measurable and profitable, even when that's not what's optimal for the people those systems serve.

This creates an interesting pattern: solutions that work brilliantly for the metrics, but create unexpected problems for humans.

Not because anyone intended harm. Because we optimized for the wrong question.

Everything As Opposite

My methodology is simple: when everyone agrees that X is the solution, I examine what happens if you try the opposite of X.

Not because conventional wisdom is wrong. Because examining opposites reveals what we're actually optimizing for versus what we think we're optimizing for.

This approach has led me from scoring award-winning documentaries to developing strategic technology concepts. More importantly, it revealed a pattern:

Many "solutions" are optimized for managing problems perpetually rather than eliminating them.

Why? Not conspiracy—economics.

Recurring revenue models favor ongoing management over one-time solutions. A problem you solve once generates less revenue than a problem you manage indefinitely.

This creates natural drift: systems evolve toward the business model, not necessarily toward the outcome.

Consider the pattern:

  • Curing a condition once = one payment. Managing it for life = recurring revenue.
  • Solving a user problem permanently = one transaction. Creating dependency = subscription model.
  • Teaching someone to fish once = one fee. Managing their hunger daily = ongoing service.

None of this requires bad actors. It's just economic gravity. Systems drift toward sustainable revenue models.

The opportunity? Design systems that align financial sustainability with optimal human outcomes.

That's harder. But it's possible.

An Example: Personal Health

I'm fifty-two. A few years ago, I had metabolic issues that the standard medical protocol said required lifelong medication management.

I wondered: what if I tried the opposite approach?

Instead of managing symptoms pharmaceutically, I addressed root causes through nutrition. The results were significant.

But here's what's interesting: this information wasn't secret. It was available. Many doctors know it works.

The challenge isn't knowledge—it's incentive structure. Medical systems are designed around recurring treatment models. Pharmaceutical companies need sustainable revenue. Insurance reimbursement favors ongoing management.

So the system naturally optimizes for: diagnose condition, prescribe medication, monitor indefinitely.

There's nothing evil about this. It's just what systems designed around recurring revenue naturally do.

The alternative—"change your diet and we won't need to see you regularly"—works medically but creates an economic problem for a system built around ongoing patient relationships.

This isn't about bad doctors. It's about system design that optimizes for different outcomes than we might prefer.

When you design for recurring revenue, you get recurring treatments.
When you design for optimal health outcomes, you might get one-time interventions.

Different goals. Different systems. Both rational within their own logic.

The Technology Pattern

Technology development follows similar patterns, but with different metrics.

Social media optimizes for engagement because that's what advertisers pay for. This isn't exploitation—it's responding to the revenue model.

Streaming platforms optimize for algorithmic efficiency because that's what scales. Not because they hate artists—because scalability determines viability.

AI development optimizes for capability demonstrations because that's what attracts funding. Not to replace humans—to prove technical feasibility.

These aren't moral failures. They're rational responses to the questions the business models ask:

  • Social media asks: "How do we monetize free content?" Answer: Engagement metrics.
  • Streaming asks: "How do we serve millions of songs instantly?" Answer: Algorithmic efficiency.
  • AI asks: "How do we demonstrate breakthrough capability?" Answer: Human-level performance benchmarks.

Each answer is logical within its economic context.

The interesting question: What if we designed for different questions?

What if social media asked: "How do we create meaningful human connection?"
What if streaming asked: "How do we serve musicians AND listeners optimally?"
What if AI asked: "How do we enhance human capability rather than replace it?"

These aren't naive questions. They're design challenges.

Technology isn't the problem. The questions we ask of technology determine what we optimize for.

The Music Industry Example

I've spent three decades as a composer. Award-winning documentaries. Festival recognition. Music that moved people.

I've also watched the music industry transform as business models changed.

The streaming era didn't fail because people stopped caring about music. It happened because the economic model asked different questions:

Previous model: "How do we sell music people love?"
Streaming model: "How do we provide access to all music for one monthly fee?"

The first model optimized for creating music people wanted to own.
The second model optimizes for maximum catalog access at minimum friction.

Neither is evil. They're rational responses to different economic questions.

But they produce different outcomes:

  • Ownership model: Fewer songs, higher per-song value, artists could sustain careers
  • Access model: Infinite catalog, per-stream micro-payments, most artists struggle

The technology isn't the problem. Streaming is remarkable engineering.

The business model optimizes for user convenience (good!) but creates economic challenges for artists (problematic).

This isn't because streaming platforms hate musicians. It's because the business model prioritizes different outcomes.

What we measure determines what we optimize for.

The opportunity: design models that serve both listeners AND creators sustainably.

The Design Question

After thirty years of composition and strategic concept development, I keep returning to one question that cuts through complexity:

"Does this help humans flourish?"

Not as moral judgment. As design criterion.

This question comes before:

  • Does this maximize profit?
  • Does this optimize efficiency?
  • Does this scale infinitely?

Those questions matter. They're necessary for sustainability.

But they serve the first question, not replace it.

When you start with "Does this help humans flourish?" and then solve for sustainable economics, you get different designs:

  • Medicine that creates health conditions where ongoing management becomes unnecessary
  • Technology that enhances human capability in ways people gladly pay for
  • Music platforms that serve both listeners and artists, creating sustainable ecosystems
  • Innovation that solves real problems, generating natural demand

This isn't profit vs. humanity. It's sequencing the questions correctly.

Ask "What serves humans best?" first.
Then ask "How do we make this economically sustainable?"

Different order. Different outcomes.

Both questions necessary. Sequence matters.

The Opportunity

I don't claim to have all answers. I have a methodology—"everything as opposite"—and three decades of applying it.

But I've observed this: Systems produce outcomes aligned with their design questions.

If you design asking "How do we maximize revenue?"—you get revenue-optimized systems.
If you design asking "How do we serve human needs sustainably?"—you get different systems.

The first approach isn't evil. It's just incomplete.

The second approach isn't naive. It's just harder to measure initially.

Long-term, systems that genuinely serve human needs create more sustainable value than systems optimized purely for extraction metrics.

Evidence? Products people love and recommend generate more lasting revenue than products that hook users through psychological tricks.

The opportunity isn't rejecting profit. It's designing systems where profit emerges naturally from genuine value creation.

That's harder to measure in quarterly reports. But it works better over decades.

An Invitation to Question

Before building anything—a business, a technology, a piece of music, a life—consider the sequence of questions:

Does this serve human flourishing?
How do we make this economically sustainable?

Not one or the other. Both. In that order.

This isn't rejecting profit. This is designing for lasting value.

Systems that genuinely serve human needs create natural demand. Natural demand generates sustainable economics.

The opposite approach—optimizing for profit metrics while hoping human value follows—works sometimes. But it creates the patterns we see: brilliant technology that somehow makes people unhappy, profitable systems that struggle with sustainability, successful businesses that leave everyone exhausted.

We can design better.

Not through moral superiority. Through better questions, asked in better sequence.

The Question

In your work, in your life, in your choices—what are you optimizing for?

What question are you asking first?

The answer shapes everything that follows.
